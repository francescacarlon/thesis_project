1. A sense (or word sense) is a distinct representation of a single meaning of a word, as understood in context. For example, "mouse" can refer to: - A computer peripheral used to interact with a system (1968 mouse) - A small rodent (rodent mouse) 2. "Bank" can have the following meanings: - A financial institution that manages and lends money (bank1) - A slope adjacent to a body of water (bank2) 3. Dictionaries and thesauruses provide alternative definitions for each sense, often in the form of glosses. 4. Synonyms are two senses of two different words that are identical or nearly identical. For example, couch and sofa are synonyms, as are filbert and hazelnut, car and automobile. 5. Antonyms are words with opposite meanings, such as long and short, big and little, fast and slow, cold and hot, dark and light. 6. A hyponym is a more specific word denoting a subclass of another word. For example, car is a hyponym of vehicle, and dog is a hyponym of animal. Conversely, vehicle is a hypernym of car, and animal is a hypernym of dog. 7. Meronymy is the part-whole relationship, for example, wheel is a part of car, and car is the whole containing wheel.

In computer science, understanding language nuances is important, especially when working with natural language processing (NLP). A "sense" or "word sense" refers to a specific meaning of a word, which can have multiple interpretations based on context. For example, consider "mouse": in one context, it refers to a computer peripheral (mouse1), while in another, it refers to a small animal (mouse2). Similarly, "bank" can mean a financial institution (bank1) or the side of a river (bank2). Dictionaries and thesauruses define these word senses using glosses, which are brief textual definitions. For instance, a "bank" can be glossed as "1. a financial institution that accepts deposits and channels the money into lending activities" or "2. sloping land beside a body of water." In NLP, it's crucial to understand relationships between word senses: - **Synonyms**: Different words with similar meanings, like couch/sofa or car/automobile. - **Antonyms**: Words with opposite meanings, such as long/short or hot/cold. - **Hyponyms and Hypernyms**: These describe subclass relationships. A "car" is a hyponym of "vehicle," meaning it’s a specific type of vehicle. Conversely, "vehicle" is a hypernym of "car." - **Meronymy**: This is the part-whole relationship. For example, a "wheel" is a meronym of "car," meaning a wheel is a part of a car, and "car" is a holonym of "wheel." These concepts help us build better algorithms for tasks like word sense disambiguation, which is crucial for developing systems that understand human language effectively.
